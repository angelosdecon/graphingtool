Utility Function U(x, y)

A utility function represents a consumer's preferences over two goods, x and y. The Cobb-Douglas form is one of the most widely used in economics.

U(x, y) = xα · y(1−α)

The partial derivatives tell us the marginal utility — how much additional satisfaction a consumer gets from one more unit of each good.

Production Function Q(K, L)

A Cobb-Douglas production function describes how capital (K) and labour (L) combine to produce output (Q).

Q(K, L) = Kα · L(1−α)

The partial derivatives give us marginal products — the additional output from one more unit of capital or labour.

What is a Derivative?

A derivative measures how fast a function changes at a particular point. Imagine you are driving — the derivative is your speedometer reading right now, not your average speed for the whole journey.

Formally, the derivative of f(x) at a point x₀ is the slope of the tangent line to the curve at that point:

f'(x) = lim[Δx→0] (f(x+Δx) − f(x)) / Δx

In economics, derivatives are everywhere: marginal cost, marginal revenue, marginal utility — all are derivatives.

  • Go to the Free Explore tab and enter a function like x²
  • Look at the 2D slice — this shows the function as a curve
  • The slope of the curve at any point is the derivative
  • Try moving the point of interest along the curve — watch the slope change

What is a Partial Derivative?

When a function depends on more than one variable, a partial derivative measures how the function changes when only one variable changes, holding all others constant.

Think of hiking on a 3D landscape. The partial derivative ∂f/∂x tells you how steep the hill is if you walk east. The partial derivative ∂f/∂y tells you how steep it is if you walk north.

∂f/∂x = rate of change of f as x changes, y held fixed

In economics: marginal utility of good x = ∂U/∂x (how much happier you get from one more unit of x, holding consumption of y constant).

  • Go to Economics tab and open the Utility Function
  • The two lower graphs show ∂U/∂x and ∂U/∂y
  • Notice each is a 2D curve — a slice through the 3D surface
  • Move the sliders and see how the slopes change

What is a Differential?

The differential is a linear approximation of how much a function changes when you move a small amount in any direction. Instead of the curved surface, you use the flat tangent plane as your approximation.

df ≈ (∂f/∂x)·dx + (∂f/∂y)·dy

In economics: if the price of good x rises by £0.10 (dx = 0.10) and good y by £0.20 (dy = 0.20), the differential tells us approximately how much total utility changes.

  • Go to Free Explore and plot any function
  • The third graph shows the tangent plane at your chosen point
  • The flat plane is the differential approximation
  • Notice how the plane fits the surface well near the point, but poorly far away
